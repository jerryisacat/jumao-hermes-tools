# Modern editorial page components

## Component selection

Every component must support a reading task. Do not add a component merely to vary the page.

## 1. Cover

Required content:

- Document label or category.
- Title.
- Optional subtitle.
- Author/organization.
- Version and date.
- Audience or scope when useful.
- Data/confidentiality/test note.

Design:

- Left aligned by default.
- Use a deliberate line break for long Chinese titles.
- One short accent rule or geometric marker is enough.
- Avoid full-page color fills and decorative illustrations.
- Fill at least roughly 60% of the usable page with meaningful content and balanced space.

## 2. Executive summary

Use one page when possible.

Suggested order:

1. Chapter label and title.
2. One-sentence framing statement.
3. Up to three metrics.
4. Three core judgments.
5. Reading/data note.
6. Compact contents list.

Metrics must contain:

- Value.
- Label.
- Short interpretation.

Do not show a metric without stating whether the data is factual or illustrative.

## 3. Contents

For short 6–8 page samples, a stable static contents block is acceptable. For production DOCX files, prefer a live TOC field based on paragraph styles with outline levels.

Contents styling:

- Keep the chapter number visually distinct.
- Align page numbers consistently.
- Use light rules or no rules.
- Avoid boxed navigation-card styling.

## 4. Chapter opening

A chapter opening contains:

- Small chapter label/number.
- Large title.
- Short accent rule.
- One-sentence introduction.

Do not force every chapter onto a mostly empty dedicated page. In reports under about 20 pages, the chapter opening normally shares a page with body content.

## 5. Narrative body

Default:

- Single column.
- One body font.
- Block paragraphs with paragraph spacing.
- Headings kept with the first following block.
- Lists used only when the ideas are genuinely parallel.

Avoid:

- A card for every paragraph.
- Replacing clear prose with dozens of fragments.
- Two-column body text that causes scanning jumps.

## 6. Metrics

Use two or three metrics in one row.

- Values use the primary accent.
- Labels use bold sans-serif.
- Notes use smaller muted text.
- The surface is light gray, with no shadow.
- Keep all metric cards the same height.

## 7. Callout

Use a callout for:

- Reading instructions.
- A decisive conclusion.
- A methodological constraint.
- A risk/warning.

Default callout:

- Soft gray surface.
- Primary blue left rule.
- Short label plus one concise paragraph.

Warning callout:

- Red left rule and red label.
- Body text remains dark gray for readability.

Do not place a callout after every section.

## 8. Charts

A chart block contains:

1. Clear title.
2. Optional one-line subtitle/method note.
3. Plot.
4. Figure number and caption.
5. Source or sample-data note.

Do not use a chart if a three-row table communicates the information more clearly.

## 9. Case study

Recommended structure:

| Background | Action | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Situation and constraint | Intervention and process | Outcome and limit |

For sample documents, mark the case as fictional. For real cases, identify source, period and attribution.

Use three parallel cells only for short content. Use stacked subsections for longer narratives.

## 10. Quote

Use a quote only when attribution or voice matters.

- Keep to one or two sentences.
- Use a larger serif style.
- Attribute clearly.
- Do not create an entire page for one quotation in a short report.

## 11. Risk table

Recommended columns:

| Risk | Typical symptom | Level | Control |
|---|---|---|---|

Use red for genuinely high risk and blue/gray for medium or informational states. Never rely on color alone; keep the textual level.

## 12. Roadmap

Recommended columns:

| Phase | Priority actions | Deliverables |
|---|---|---|

A blue header is acceptable when the roadmap is the main action component. Keep time ranges and deliverables concrete.

## 13. Appendix

Use the appendix for:

- Methodology.
- Data definitions.
- Sample-data disclaimer.
- Component test coverage.
- Source list.
- Glossary.

The appendix should finish the document with useful information, not serve as visual filler.
